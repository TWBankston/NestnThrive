What to Look for When Choosing a Standing Desk

Size & Fit

In a small apartment, depth is often the enemy. A standard office desk is 30 inches deep, which can dominate a bedroom. Look for "compact" models that are 24 inches deep. Widths usually start at 42 inches—anything smaller than that makes it difficult to fit a chair underneath comfortably.

Motor Quality & Noise

Single motor desks are cheaper but slower and often noisier (around 60dB). Dual motor desks are quieter (40-50dB) and smoother, which is important if you share your space with a partner or roommate. If you adjust your desk frequently, the dual motor upgrade is worth it.

Stability (The Wobble Factor)

Every standing desk wobbles a little at max height. However, heavier frames with wider feet mitigate this. Desks with a "crossbar" (a horizontal bar connecting the legs) are the most stable but can limit legroom. C-frame legs (where the leg is set back) offer more knee room but slightly less stability than T-frame legs.

Materials

Laminate is the standard—it's durable and easy to clean. Bamboo is eco-friendly and harder than wood but can be sensitive to humidity changes. Solid wood is beautiful but heavy and expensive; avoid it for budget setups as it requires a very strong motor frame to lift.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a standing desk worth it for home use?
Yes, mostly for the ability to change posture. Even if you only stand for 30 minutes a day, the ability to fine-tune the seated height to match your body perfectly is often worth the cost alone.
How high should my desk be?
Your elbows should be at a 90-degree angle when typing. For a 5'9" person, this is usually around 44" high when standing and 27" high when seated, though it varies by individual proportions.
Can I use my own desktop with these frames?
Absolutely. Brands like Flexispot and Uplift sell "frame-only" kits. You can buy a butcher block countertop from a hardware store and screw it onto the frame for a custom high-end look at a lower price.
Do I need a cable management tray?
Highly recommended. Since the desk moves up and down, loose cables can get snagged or pulled. A tray keeps everything tucked under the desk so only one power cord goes to the wall.
