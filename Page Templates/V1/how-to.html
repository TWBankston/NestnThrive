How to Ergonomically Set Up Your Desk (Step-by-Step)

Stop the afternoon back pain. This guide breaks down exactly how to position your chair, monitor, and keyboard to work comfortably for hours, even in a small space.

By Elena Miles
5 Min Read Updated Oct 12, 2023

In This Guide

  • The "90-Degree Rule" for elbows and knees
  • How to find the perfect monitor height to prevent neck strain
  • Adjusting lighting to reduce eye fatigue

Most of us don't think about ergonomics until something hurts. But setting up your workspace correctly isn't just about preventing pain—it’s about creating an environment where focus comes naturally. The goal isn't to buy the most expensive chair, but to align the tools you have with your body's natural posture.

1

Start with the Chair (The Foundation)

Before you touch your monitor or keyboard, you need to be seated correctly. Your feet should be flat on the floor (or a footrest) with your knees bent at a 90-degree angle.

Adjust your seat height until your thighs are parallel to the floor. If your feet dangle, you're putting pressure on the back of your legs, which reduces circulation.

Pro Tip

If your desk is too high and your feet don't reach the floor when your arms are comfortable, prioritize your arms. Use a footrest to bring the floor up to you.

2

The Monitor Height "High-Five"

Neck strain usually comes from looking down at a laptop or up at a monitor mounted too high. The top of your screen should be at or slightly below eye level.

When you look at the middle of the screen, your eyes should be looking slightly downward. Extend your arm—your monitor should be roughly an arm's length away.

3

Elbows and Keyboards

Your elbows should remain by your sides, bent at roughly 90 to 100 degrees. If you have to reach forward to type, you are engaging your shoulder muscles unnecessarily.

Keep the mouse close to the keyboard. A compact keyboard (without the number pad) allows you to keep the mouse closer to your body’s centerline, reducing shoulder strain.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The "Turtle Neck"

Leaning your head forward to read small text. Instead of leaning in, increase your font size or move the monitor closer.

Wrist Anchoring

Resting wrists heavily on the desk while typing creates pressure on the carpal tunnel. Hover your hands slightly or use a soft palm rest.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is standing better than sitting?

Not necessarily. The key is movement. Standing all day can hurt your knees and lower back just as sitting hurts your hips. Ideally, switch positions every 45-60 minutes.

Do I need an ergonomic chair?

An adjustable chair helps significantly, but you can hack a standard chair with a lumbar pillow and a seat cushion. The most important feature is height adjustability.
